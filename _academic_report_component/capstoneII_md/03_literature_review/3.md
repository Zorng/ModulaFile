# Literature Review

## 3.1 Overview of the Literature Review

The purpose of this literature review is to examine existing Point of Sale (POS) systems and related software concepts in order to support the development of Modula. The chapter first reviews the basic ideas behind modern POS systems and the modules they commonly provide. It then looks at selected international and local POS solutions to understand what is already available in practice, where current systems are strong, and where important limitations still remain.

This review is important because Modula was not designed in isolation. The project needed to understand both mainstream POS practice and local operating realities, especially in areas such as payment methods, branch operation, inventory handling, and system flexibility. By reviewing these systems, the chapter identifies both useful capabilities and gaps that influenced the design direction of the project.

Capstone II also adds an important extension to the literature basis of the project. During implementation, it became clear that feature coverage alone was not enough. Once the system was treated more seriously as an operational product, issues such as unstable connectivity, retries, partial failures, and synchronization consistency became central design concerns. For that reason, this chapter also includes a short review of reliability patterns relevant to offline-first and distributed POS systems [16][17][18].

## 3.2 Foundation of POS Systems and Modular Software

### 3.2.1 Introduction to POS

A Point of Sale (POS) refers to the location or moment where a sale occurs, marking the transfer of goods from the retailer to the customer and accounting for applicable taxes. A POS system extends this concept by providing software-driven management of sales operations, offering real-time control over inventory, sales analysis, and near-real-time insights to support informed decision-making [1].

Historically, POS systems were limited to fixed cash registers and manual inventory tracking. While sufficient for simple transactions, these systems lacked flexibility and real-time data processing. Technological advancements have transformed POS solutions into mobile and cloud-based systems, offering greater speed, mobility, and centralized access to data. Modern POS integrates wireless connectivity (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth), cloud computing, and advanced applications such as sales tracking, inventory management, and analytics, enabling businesses to operate more efficiently [2].

In business, particularly in retail, restaurants, and cafés, POS systems streamline transactions, improve operational efficiency, and support data-driven decision-making. In the Food and Beverage (F&B) industry, they accelerate order processing, optimize food production, reduce service and payment times, and enhance overall operational control, which is crucial for meeting customer demands and managing high-volume operations effectively [3].

### 3.2.2 Core Modules of Modern POS Systems

Modern POS systems are designed to support business operations efficiently by integrating essential modules that handle sales, inventory, staff management, and reporting. These core modules include:

- Inventory tracking: monitors stock levels, provides alerts for low inventory, and supports restocking workflows.
- Sales and transaction management: supports accurate sales processing and multiple payment methods.
- Employee management: tracks staff attendance, schedules, and activity reports.
- Reporting and analytics: generates operational summaries and supports data-driven decision-making. [4, 5, 6, 7]

In addition to feature modules, modern cloud-based POS systems increasingly require reliability primitives such as offline operation support, synchronization, auditability, and retry-safe operation. These topics are addressed in the Capstone II extension section later in this chapter.

### 3.2.3 Analysis of Existing POS Systems

This section examines selected international and local POS systems to understand current POS implementations, system features, and practical limitations in real business environments.

#### 3.2.3.1 International POS Systems

##### 1. Square POS system

Square POS is a widely used point-of-sale system designed for small and medium-sized businesses, particularly in retail and café environments. Its core features include:

- Payment processing: supports multiple payment methods, including card-based, contactless, and digital payments.
- Order management and cashier interface: provides a cashier-oriented interface for placing and tracking orders.
- Sales reporting and analytics: provides dashboards with sales data, transaction history, and performance reports.
- Inventory management: includes basic inventory tracking tools.
- Staff management: includes employee onboarding, time tracking, scheduling, and staff administration. [4]

Despite its comprehensive features, Square POS has some limitations. Its all-in-one design offers limited customization, restricting flexibility for businesses with complex workflows. Additionally, as a cloud-based platform, it relies heavily on stable internet connectivity; real-time reporting and synchronization may be disrupted in environments with unreliable connectivity. [8]

##### 2. Toast POS

Toast POS is a cloud-based point-of-sale system designed for foodservice businesses of various sizes. Its core features include:

- Sales reporting and analytics: real-time dashboards and summaries accessible via mobile applications.
- Inventory management: tracks product availability and supports menu/inventory linkage.
- Offline mode: allows continued transaction processing when internet connectivity is unavailable.
- Payment processing: supports multiple payment methods, including contactless and digital payments.
- Order management and cashier interface: supports order taking, tracking, and fulfillment flows. [5, 9]

Despite its functionality, Toast POS setup can be time-consuming, and advanced features may require add-on subscriptions, increasing costs for smaller businesses. [10]

#### 3.2.3.2 Local POS Systems

##### 1. Champei POS

Champei POS is a Cambodian point-of-sale solution that can be customized for different store types. For cafés, its core features include:

- Multi-branch management: supports managing multiple outlets and aggregated sales summaries.
- Sales reporting: generates daily summaries and supports real-time access via mobile devices.
- Employee permission system: supports staff roles and duties.
- KHQR payment integration: supports QR-based payments in Cambodia. [11]

Champei POS does not emphasize inventory management on its official website, suggesting a reduced feature scope for stock tracking.

##### 2. MPOS

MPOS is a Cambodian POS system designed for restaurants and cafés. Its core features include:

- Cashier management: supports multiple payment methods and dual-currency transactions.
- Order management: supports dine-in and takeaway orders with status tracking.
- Inventory management: supports procurement, stock tracking, and recipe-linked deduction for accurate cost control. [12]

### 3.2.3 Discussion of POS Features and Identified Gaps

Based on the core features of modern POS systems, the reviewed international and local solutions demonstrate different levels of feature support, flexibility, and operational maturity.

International POS systems generally provide strong support for sales processing, inventory, staff management, and reporting. In many cases, they are mature and operationally polished. However, they may not fit local workflows well, and they often assume stronger infrastructure conditions than small businesses can guarantee consistently.

Local POS systems are often better aligned with regional needs, especially around payment standards and practical business operation. However, some of them appear to provide narrower feature depth, weaker inventory support, or less architectural flexibility when compared with larger international systems.

The main gap identified through this review is not simply “missing features.” It is the difficulty of combining several desirable properties at the same time:
- strong operational coverage,
- flexibility for different business sizes and workflows,
- support for localized payment and branch realities,
- and resilience under unreliable connectivity.

This gap is one of the reasons Modula adopts a modular direction. The goal is not only to include core POS functions, but to organize them in a way that can remain flexible, locally relevant, and technically reliable as the system grows.

### 3.2.4 Modular Software Concept

Modular software is a design approach in which a system is built from independent and interchangeable modules, each responsible for a specific function. These modules communicate through clearly defined interfaces, enabling changes or extensions to be made without affecting the entire system [13][14].

The key advantages of modular software include flexibility, scalability, and ease of maintenance. Modular architecture allows features to be introduced gradually through phased implementation, while updates and fixes can be applied to individual modules instead of the whole system. This reduces system complexity and supports long-term adaptability to evolving requirements [13][15].

## 3.3 Reliability Patterns for Modern Cloud and Offline-First POS (Capstone II Extension)

While modular feature coverage is necessary, it is not enough for a POS system that must operate in real business conditions. Once implementation began to address offline use, retries, and cross-module effects, it became clear that reliability patterns had to be treated as part of the design basis rather than as optional backend details.

In practice, a POS system behaves like a distributed system [17]:
- clients and servers operate on different devices and networks,
- connectivity can be intermittent,
- retries and duplicate delivery occur naturally,
- and cross-module side effects can be interrupted by partial failures.

For this reason, Capstone II extends the design basis of Modula to include patterns that improve safety and correctness under these conditions.

### 3.3.1 Offline-First Synchronization (Replay + Authoritative Hydration)

In modern client applications, offline-first behavior requires local and remote data to be coordinated through explicit synchronization logic rather than treated as a simple cache [18]. In a modern POS, this requirement goes beyond a simple “offline mode.” The system must support convergence between:
- the device’s local state (used for continuity of operation), and
- the server’s authoritative state (used for integrity, auditability, and cross-device correctness).

Typical patterns include:
- durable offline operation queues,
- deterministic replay using client operation identifiers,
- and authoritative pull/hydration via ordered change feeds (cursor-based), including tombstones for hard removals.

### 3.3.2 Idempotency Under Retries

Under unreliable networks, clients may retry requests and servers may receive duplicates. Without idempotency, the same user action may be applied more than once, creating serious problems such as double-finalized sales, duplicated cash movements, or repeated printing.

Idempotency keys enable the system to apply a write intent exactly once and return the same result on retries, improving safety without depending on perfect connectivity [16].

### 3.3.3 Outbox Pattern for Reliable Side Effects

A POS write operation often triggers downstream side effects such as inventory deduction, notifications, printing, and reporting updates. If these effects are executed without a reliable boundary, partial failure can leave the system in an inconsistent intermediate state.

The outbox pattern is a common approach to ensure reliability [17]:
- business truth is written to the database,
- an outbox record is written in the same transaction,
- and a background worker processes outbox records to perform side effects reliably with retry safety and idempotency.
