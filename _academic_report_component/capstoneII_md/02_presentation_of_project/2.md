# 2. Presentation of Project

## 2.1 General Presentation of the Project

Modula is a modular Point-of-Sale (POS) system for small and medium-sized businesses. The project started in Capstone I and continues in Capstone II, but the current phase is no longer only about showing that a POS can be built. It is about turning the earlier prototype into something that behaves more like a real operational system.

One important decision in the project is to keep Modula as a **web-based POS** that works on both **small screens** such as smartphones and **wide screens** such as tablets and desktops. This choice matters because many small businesses do not want to begin with expensive dedicated hardware. At the same time, the system still needs to support wider operational setups once the business grows or the work becomes more structured.

A second important idea behind Modula is that it is **modular by design**. The system is not treated as one fixed bundle where every business must use the same features in the same way. Instead, it is organized into modules and capability layers so a business can begin with the core operating features and adopt additional behavior only when needed. This is one of the reasons the project later became closely tied to subscription-aware thinking and capability gating.

Although Modula is being developed first around the Food and Beverage (F&B) domain, the project is not intended to stop as a single-purpose restaurant application. The design direction is closer to a foundation that can support multiple business workflows through clear module boundaries, policy-driven behavior, and branch-scoped operations.

The current state of the project is shaped heavily by what happened after the Capstone I prototype was exercised more seriously. The earlier prototype was useful, but it behaved more like a feature showcase than a stable operating product. In Capstone II, that changed. The frontend was reorganized around clearer workflow navigation. The system now distinguishes account, tenant, and branch usage layers more explicitly. It also supports both mobile and wide-screen operation more intentionally, instead of treating small-screen flow as the only important case.

Capstone II also added or deepened several practical capabilities that were necessary once the system was treated as a real product rather than only a prototype. These include stronger account onboarding through phone-based OTP, real KHQR payment handling with backend verification, GPS-supported attendance evidence, operational notifications, pay-later workflow support, and automatic stock deduction linked to finalized sales. Subscription-aware billing was also designed and prepared, although not all of that work could be implemented fully within the available time.

Overall, Modula in this report should be understood as a modular POS system that is still under development, but already far beyond the stage of a conceptual prototype. Capstone II mainly strengthens the foundations, the workflow model, and the implementation credibility of the project.

## 2.2 Problems

Small and medium-sized businesses often operate with fragmented tools, informal procedures, or manual records. In that kind of setting, problems do not only appear in reporting. They also appear in daily service work, staff accountability, and business growth.

Key problems include:
- **Fragmented workflows and manual processes**: sales, inventory, and staff operations are often tracked using paper or disconnected tools, increasing human error and reducing visibility.
- **Inventory mismanagement**: without systematic tracking, businesses struggle to maintain accurate inventory levels, leading to waste, shortages, and inconsistent reporting.
- **High cost of adoption**: many POS solutions require costly hardware and bundled feature plans that do not match the needs of small businesses.
- **Lack of flexibility**: businesses evolve over time (new branches, new roles, new workflows). Systems that are rigid or monolithic are difficult to adapt without high switching costs.
- **Unreliable connectivity and real-world constraints**: network reliability cannot be assumed. A POS system must be resilient to intermittent connectivity without compromising data integrity.

These are the conditions that motivate Modula. The system is intended to respond not only to the need for digitization, but also to the need for a system that is affordable, flexible, branch-aware, and able to keep working under unstable network conditions.

## 2.3 Target Users

Modula is not designed as a personal productivity application. It is designed as a business system shared by multiple people inside the same organization. Because of that, different users do not enter the system with the same purpose or the same authority. Distinguishing those users is important because much of the project design depends on role, scope, and branch responsibility.

Another useful way to understand the target users is through two operating patterns that appeared clearly during the project. Some businesses operate in a **solo mode**, where the owner runs the branch personally and does not need a full staff-management workflow. Other businesses operate in a **team mode**, where multiple people work under the same business and the system must support delegation, attendance, branch assignment, and role-sensitive access. This distinction became important in Capstone II because it affected both product packaging and system behavior.

### 2.3.1 Business Owners (Primary Customers)

Business owners are the primary customers of Modula. They are responsible for creating or controlling the business workspace, deciding which capabilities should be enabled, and determining how the system is used across branches. In some cases, the owner is also the main operator of the branch. This corresponds to the solo-operator pattern, where the business may run with the core POS workflow and without a larger team-management setup. In other cases, the owner manages a team and delegates daily work to other users. That corresponds to team mode.

### 2.3.2 Delegated Operators (Administrators and Managers)

Delegated operators, such as administrators and managers, work on behalf of the owner. They appear mainly in team-mode operation, where the branch no longer depends on one person handling everything. These users are closer to daily operations and are usually responsible for configuration, supervision, and staff-related work within the limits of their assigned authority.

### 2.3.3 Frontline Staff (Cashiers and Operational Employees)

Frontline staff, such as cashiers and other operational employees, use Modula mainly for service execution. Their work includes tasks such as attendance actions, cash-session participation, order handling, and sale-related workflows. Their access is operational and branch-scoped rather than administrative. In practice, this user group becomes relevant once the business moves from solo operation into team operation, because that is when branch-level accountability and staff-specific workflows become necessary.

### Summary

Across these user groups, Modula operates as a shared organizational platform. The solo-operator versus team-mode distinction helps explain why the system must support both a simpler path for small businesses and a more structured path for businesses with multiple operators. That is also why the project needed clearer multi-tenant membership handling, explicit tenant and branch scopes, and stronger role-aware behavior in both backend rules and frontend workflows.

## 2.4 Objectives

The development of Modula is guided by objectives that are both academic and practical. On one side, the project must satisfy the capstone requirement by showing analysis, design, implementation, and documentation. On the other side, it must still make sense as a real POS product under realistic operating conditions.

The main objective is to design and develop a modular POS system for small and medium-sized businesses while correcting the structural weaknesses that became visible in the earlier prototype. In practical terms, this means the project must not stop at “feature existence.” It must show that the system can be organized, implemented, and evolved in a technically defensible way.

To support that main goal, the project works through several specific objectives:

1. **Deliver a modular POS foundation**

   Define clear module boundaries and cross-module contracts so the system can evolve without becoming tightly coupled.

2. **Support multi-user organizational operation**

   Model owners, delegated operators, and frontline staff with clear authorization boundaries and traceability of actions.

3. **Support offline-first operation without compromising integrity**

   Enable resilient operation under unstable connectivity through queued actions, deterministic replay, and authoritative hydration patterns.

4. **Maintain a responsive interface across device sizes**

   Ensure core workflows remain usable on both small screens (smartphones) and wide screens (tablets/desktops).

5. **Demonstrate alignment between documentation, design, and implementation**

   Maintain consistency between requirements, specifications, architecture decisions, and implemented behavior, reflecting realistic software engineering practice.

6. **Improve production-oriented operational readiness**

   Move beyond prototype assumptions by supporting real operational capabilities such as verified account onboarding, KHQR-assisted payment workflows, attendance evidence, inventory deduction behavior, and staged deployment.

## 2.5 Planning of the Project

The planning of Modula POS in Capstone II is phased, but it is not rigid. The project had to adapt as implementation revealed more about the real difficulty of the system. Compared to Capstone I, the main planning change is that Capstone II is less about demonstrating isolated features and more about correcting architecture, stabilizing workflows, and making the system ready for integration and staged use.

This reprioritization did not happen in the abstract. It came from the prototype itself. Once the prototype was pushed further, it became clear that some important assumptions were still too weak: authentication and membership flow, workspace separation, offline-first behavior, cross-module consistency, and several backend reliability concerns. Because of that, Capstone II focused on improving the internal coherence of the system before continuing unchecked feature expansion.

At the same time, Capstone II still introduced several additions that were necessary for real operation, including notifications, KHQR payment handling with backend verification, phone-based OTP onboarding, GPS-supported attendance evidence, automatic inventory deduction, pay-later workflow support, and preparation for subscription-aware billing. Some billing work remained partially deferred because the implementation effort turned out to be larger than expected compared with the available time and the team’s capacity.

### 2.5.1 Planning Structure and Timeline

The project is planned using overlapping phases:
- **System and specification refinement** (architecture and KB alignment)
- **UI/UX refinement** (responsive behavior across phone/tablet/desktop and clearer workspace navigation)
- **Backend and frontend development** (module-by-module delivery)
- **Integration and stabilization** (end-to-end wiring and pilot readiness)

### 2.5.4 Planning Adaptation and Specification Refinement

A defining feature of the project planning process is continuous refinement based on implementation feedback. In Capstone II, this became necessary because several rules that looked acceptable at the prototype stage were not precise enough once frontend and backend work had to align against the same system behavior.

Examples of refinement include:
- clarifying multi-tenant membership and invitation flows,
- restructuring frontend workflows around explicit account, tenant, and branch layers,
- redesigning responsive navigation for both mobile and wide-screen operation,
- aligning branch monetization semantics to paid activation,
- formalizing offline-first synchronization beyond replay (authoritative pull/hydration),
- clarifying rule overlap and lifecycle constraints in operational configurations (e.g., discount rules),
- strengthening lifecycle rules for inventory deduction, attendance capability gating, and subscription-aware operational behavior.

These refinements improved feasibility, reduced ambiguity, and made the system more realistic to integrate and test. In other words, planning in Capstone II was not only about scheduling work. It was also about tightening the rules of the system until implementation and specification could support each other properly.
